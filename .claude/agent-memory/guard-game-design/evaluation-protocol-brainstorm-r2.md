## The Lens

The designer clarified the real distinction:

- **Chips** are **power**. They make you stronger within your current playstyle. They can be simple stat boosts or complex triggered effects. But they don't change how you WANT to play.
- **Protocols** are **rule changes**. They make you WANT to do something non-standard. Without the protocol, you play normally. With it, you deliberately do things you normally wouldn't.

**The test**: Does this make me play differently, or just play the same way but stronger?

## Reclassifications: 5 Protocols Demoted to Chips

### 1. Overburn (was Protocol 5) -> CHIP

**Why it fails the test**: Overburn gives your bolt a damage aura. You don't WANT to do anything differently -- you already want the bolt to travel through dense cell areas. The bolt just does extra damage automatically along its existing path. That's POWER. The "navigate, don't aim" pitch sounds like a play-style shift, but at the breaker level, you're still bumping, still aiming. The bolt just has an aura now.

**As a chip**: This is a great legendary chip. "Bolt damages cells within a small radius of its path." Put it in the chip pool with the Momentum hazard trap intact. It makes damage builds stronger without changing their approach.

### 2. Fission (was Protocol 8) -> CHIP

**Why it fails the test**: Every 8th cell destroyed splits a bolt. But you already want to destroy cells. Fission rewards what you're already doing and gives you more bolts. You don't WANT to do anything non-standard -- you just get rewarded for playing normally. That's POWER.

**As a chip**: Legendary chip. "Every 8th cell destroyed permanently splits a bolt." The Chain Reaction synergy makes it a build-defining legendary that rewards destruction volume. Still interesting, just not a rule change.

### 3. Iron Curtain (was Protocol 9) -> CHIP

**Why it fails the test**: Bolt-lost spawns a shield wall + damages all cells. This is a consolation prize, not a behavior change. You don't WANT to lose bolts more often. You still want to catch them. The safety net doesn't change your incentives -- it just makes failure less painful. That's defensive POWER.

**As a chip**: Legendary chip. "When a bolt is lost, spawn a timed shield wall and damage all cells." Synergizes with Aegis breaker and Desperation chip. Good defensive legendary that competes for chip slots honestly.

### 4. Kickstart (was Protocol 10) -> CHIP

**Why it fails the test**: 3s of 2x speed + 2x damage at node start. You already want to optimize your opening. Kickstart makes the first 3 seconds stronger, but you don't WANT to do anything you weren't already doing. You were already going to bump the bolt into cells as fast as possible. This is POWER with a timer.

**As a chip**: Legendary chip. "Node starts with 3s of double bolt speed and double bolt damage." Clean, dramatic, easy to understand. The visual contrast between powered and unpowered states makes great juice. But it doesn't change your strategy.

### 5. Gravity Lens (was Protocol 12) -> CHIP

**Why it fails the test**: Bolt curves toward dense clusters scaled by speed. The "aim at wrong angles knowing gravity corrects" pitch sounds like different play, but it's actually auto-aim that scales with speed. You're playing the same way but aiming is easier. That's POWER. The bolt goes where you want it to go with less effort. A master who can already aim precisely doesn't need this; a novice gets free accuracy. This is power that helps everyone equally -- which is a yellow flag from the skill ceiling pillar.

**As a chip**: Legendary chip. "Bolt curves toward the densest cell cluster. Curvature strength scales with speed." Interesting build-around for speed builds. The Overcharge hazard interaction (positive feedback loop) is still compelling.

## Protocols That Pass the Test (10 remaining)

### Confirmed Protocols

| # | Protocol | Why It's a Rule Change |
|---|----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Deadline | You WANT to slow-play the first 75% of nodes. Inverts pacing instinct. |
| 2 | Ricochet Protocol | You WANT to aim for walls, not cells. Inverts aiming instinct. |
| 3 | Convergence Engine | You WANT to destroy cells in a specific order (outside-in). Introduces spatial puzzle. |
| 4 | Undertow | You WANT to bump every 1-2 seconds. Transforms game rhythm. |
| 6 | Debt Collector | You WANT to whiff and lose bolts deliberately. Inverts failure instinct. |
| 7 | Bloodline | You WANT to target hard cells first. Inverts target priority. |
| 11 | Echo Strike | You WANT to perfect-bump at specific LOCATIONS, not just times. Adds spatial dimension to bump timing. |
| 13 | Siphon | You WANT to prioritize AoE kills to farm time. Timer becomes a resource, not a threat. |
| 14 | Overclock Protocol | You WANT to protect your stack by being conservative about risky bumps. Creates tower-building tension. |
| 15 | Tier Regression | You WANT to retreat instead of advance. Inverts progression instinct. (Weakest, but passes.) |

### Notes on Borderline Cases

**Bloodline**: Borderline. You're still bumping and aiming at cells. But TARGET PRIORITY is a fundamental strategic shift -- threading through to hit the hardest cell first instead of clearing convenient ones. The "protect your powered-up bolt" tension is also a real play-pattern change. Passes.

**Overclock Protocol**: Borderline. The whiff-reset creates a "protect my investment" instinct that genuinely changes risk-taking behavior. You avoid risky dash-cancels you'd normally attempt. The size growth changes bolt physics in unexpected ways. Passes, but barely.

**Tier Regression**: Weakest protocol. It's a one-time strategic decision, not ongoing play-different. But the choice to RETREAT is so fundamentally non-standard that it earns its spot. The post-regression experience is identical to normal play. This should remain a protocol but should never be the template for future protocol design.

## 5 Replacement Protocols

Each must pass the test: "I WANT to do something I normally wouldn't."

### New Protocol A: Greed

**One-line**: Skipping chip offerings permanently increases all bolt damage by 20%.

**Strategy shift**: You WANT to refuse chips. Every chip selection screen becomes a genuine decision: "Is this chip worth more than a permanent 20% damage stack?" In a normal run, you always take a chip. With Greed, sometimes the best play is to walk away with nothing. The power curve inverts -- the fewer chips you take, the more raw damage your bolt has, but the fewer synergies you can build. Pure damage vs. synergy engine.

**Why it's a rule change**: You deliberately do something you'd never do otherwise (skip a chip). Every offering screen changes from "which chip?" to "chip or damage?" That's a fundamentally different decision tree.

**Synergies**:
- **Damage Boost chips** (if you take them): Multiplicative with Greed stacks. A Savage DamageBoost (1.35x) on a bolt with 3 Greed stacks (1.728x) = 2.33x total. But you gave up 3 chips to get there -- was the raw damage better than 3 synergy chips?
- **Bloodline protocol** (cannot take both): Both reward damage scaling, different axis. Good niche differentiation.
- **Singularity chip**: If the ONE chip you take is Singularity, you have a tiny fast bolt with massive base damage from Greed stacks + 2.5x from Singularity. The "one perfect chip" build.
- **Decay hazard**: Timer pressure makes the chip-skip decision harder. Less time to deliberate on already-fraught offerings.
- **Erosion hazard**: Fewer chips means fewer breaker buffs. Breaker stays skinny. High damage bolt but hard to catch.

**Trap potential**: Looks like free power. But skipping chips means no AoE, no piercing, no triggered effects, no synergies. By tier 6, a bolt with 3x base damage but no piercing, no shockwaves, no chain lightning struggles against multi-HP cells in dense layouts. Raw damage isn't enough when you need area coverage. Greed is phenomenal for single-target precision builds and terrible for AoE/chaos builds.

**Floor/ceiling**: Beginners skip everything and have a big-damage bolt with no tools. Experts calculate exactly which chips to keep and which to skip, building a razor-thin kit of 2-3 synergy chips backed by massive base damage.

**Unlock tier**: Mid. The math of "when is 20% damage worth more than a chip?" requires catalog knowledge.

### New Protocol B: Reckless Dash

**One-line**: Bolt impacts during your dash deal 4x damage. Bolt-lost during dash costs an extra life.

**Strategy shift**: You WANT to be dashing when the bolt hits cells. Normally, dash is a repositioning tool -- you dash to get somewhere. With Reckless Dash, dash is a damage amplifier. You're timing dashes to coincide with bolt-cell impacts, which means you need to predict bolt trajectory AND time your dash so you're mid-dash when impact occurs. The extra-life penalty on bolt-loss during dash creates razor tension: dashing makes your bolt lethal but makes losing it devastating.

**Why it's a rule change**: Dash goes from a movement ability to a damage trigger. You WANT to be dashing constantly, but the penalty makes every dash a calculated risk. The decision "should I dash right now?" goes from "do I need to be somewhere else?" to "is the bolt about to hit a cell AND can I avoid losing it?"

**Synergies**:
- **Breaker Speed chips**: Faster dash = more time spent in dash state = more damage windows. But also more time in the "extra penalty" danger zone.
- **Piercing Shot chips**: 4x damage + piercing = one dashed cell impact clears a column. Devastating alpha strikes.
- **Desperation chip**: Bolt-lost = speed boost. With Reckless Dash's extra penalty (life + time), Desperation's speed boost partially offsets by helping you catch the respawned bolt. Risk-reward feedback loop.
- **Erosion hazard**: Smaller breaker = harder to catch bolt during dash recovery. The penalty for bolt-loss during dash becomes more likely as erosion stacks. Pressure compound.
- **Haste hazard**: Faster bolt = harder to predict impact timing during dash. Miscalculations become more likely.
- **Chrono breaker**: Time penalty on bolt-loss stacks with the extra life cost. Devastating. But the damage multiplier might be worth the risk for aggressive Chrono players.

**Trap potential**: Looks like free 4x damage. But maintaining dash state while also tracking bolt trajectory is mechanically demanding. Players who dash randomly will lose bolts during dash and hemorrhage lives. The extra-life penalty is brutal -- at 1 life remaining, dashing at all is a run-ending gamble. Also terrible against Erosion -- smaller breaker means harder bump recovery after dash, leading to more bolt-loss during the danger window.

**Floor/ceiling**: Beginners dash randomly, lose extra lives, die fast. Experts track bolt trajectory, time dashes to coincide with cell impacts, and deal 4x damage on every hit while never losing a bolt during dash. The gap between these two players is enormous.

**Unlock tier**: Mid. Requires dash mastery and bolt-tracking ability, both intermediate skills.

### New Protocol C: Polarity

**One-line**: Bolt alternates between "positive" (blue) and "negative" (red) charge on each bump. Positive deals 3x to cells on the right half; negative deals 3x to cells on the left half. Uncharged half takes 0.5x.

**Strategy shift**: You WANT to aim left or right depending on the bolt's current charge. Every bump becomes a targeting decision based on polarity: odd bumps go right, even bumps go left. The entire field is split into "correct" and "wrong" targets that flip every bump. You're constantly planning two bumps ahead: "this bump sends it right for 3x, the next bump will be negative so I need the bolt to come back for a left-side hit."

**Why it's a rule change**: You deliberately avoid hitting cells on the "wrong" side. Without Polarity, every cell is equally valid. With it, half the field is always the wrong target. This creates a rhythm: right-left-right-left, forcing you to plan multi-bump sequences. You WANT to skip cells you'd normally hit because they're on the wrong side.

**Synergies**:
- **Ricochet Protocol** (cannot take both): Both change aiming strategy. Good niche separation.
- **Piercing Shot chips**: 3x damage + piercing on the "correct" side = devastating column clears. But piercing through to the "wrong" side wastes the 3x.
- **Bolt Speed chips**: Faster bolt means less time to adjust aim between polarity flips. High speed + Polarity demands fast decision-making.
- **Prism breaker**: Extra bolts on perfect bump. Multiple bolts can hit different sides -- but they all share the same polarity. Multi-bolt on the correct side is great; multi-bolt on the wrong side is wasted damage.
- **Drift hazard**: Wind pushes bolt sideways, potentially into the wrong-side cells. Fighting drift while managing polarity is a dual-axis challenge.
- **Diffusion hazard**: Damage shares to neighbors. If a "correct side" cell borders the center line, Diffusion bleeds into "wrong side" cells at 0.5x. Slightly awkward interaction.

**Trap potential**: Terrible on asymmetric layouts where all the high-value targets are on one side. If the tough cells are all on the right and you bump at the wrong polarity, you're stuck dealing 0.5x to the targets you need to kill. Also bad with AoE builds -- shockwaves and chain lightning don't respect polarity boundaries (or do they? Design decision needed), so AoE might nullify the mechanic entirely. If AoE ignores polarity, this protocol is only for precision builds.

**Design note**: Shockwave, chain lightning, and explosion damage should respect polarity (deal 3x or 0.5x depending on which half the affected cell is on). Otherwise AoE ignores the core mechanic. This makes AoE builds interesting with Polarity rather than nullifying it -- you want AoE to trigger on the correct side.

**Floor/ceiling**: Beginners hit whatever they can and deal 0.5x half the time. Experts plan multi-bump sequences that consistently hit the correct side, effectively dealing 3x on every cell impact.

**Unlock tier**: Mid. Simple concept (left/right targeting), complex execution.

### New Protocol D: Momentum Flip

**One-line**: Every 4th consecutive cell hit without a wall bounce, the bolt reverses direction (180-degree turn). Wall bounces reset the counter.

**Strategy shift**: You WANT to line up straight-line paths through cells so the bolt hits 4 cells, reverses, and hits them again. You're threading the bolt through cell formations for double passes. Normally you're happy with any cell hit. With Momentum Flip, you WANT to avoid wall bounces (to maintain the counter) and aim for dense straight lines. Wall bounces, which are normally neutral or positive, become something you actively avoid.

**Why it's a rule change**: You WANT to avoid wall bounces. In normal play, wall bounces are neutral events. With Momentum Flip, they reset your counter and waste a reversal opportunity. You're aiming for cell-only paths and avoiding walls. This is almost the inverse of Ricochet Protocol (which wants wall bounces). Also, you WANT to hit exactly 4 cells in a line, which changes how you angle every bump.

**Synergies**:
- **Piercing Shot chips**: The core synergy. Piercing lets the bolt pass through cells in a line, counting toward the 4-hit reversal. 3 Piercing + Momentum Flip = bolt goes through 4 cells, reverses, goes through them again. 8 hits per pass.
- **Damage Boost chips**: Every cell gets hit twice per pass. Multiplicative damage stacks on double-hit paths are devastating.
- **Ricochet Protocol** (cannot take both): Perfect opposition. Ricochet wants walls. Momentum Flip avoids walls. Good niche separation.
- **Overcharge hazard**: Speed ramps per kill within a bump cycle. The reversal keeps the bolt in the cell field (no wall bounces to break the cycle), so speed ramps continuously. Sounds like a buff until the bolt becomes untraceable.
- **Fracture hazard**: More cells = more targets in the path = easier to maintain the 4-hit counter. Debris cells help.
- **Drift hazard**: Wind pushes bolt sideways, potentially into walls. Drift directly fights Momentum Flip's wall-avoidance strategy.

**Trap potential**: Terrible on sparse layouts where you can't hit 4 cells without a wall bounce. Also bad if you can't aim precisely enough to thread cell lines -- the bolt hits 2 cells, bounces off a wall, counter resets, no reversal ever fires. Requires Piercing to truly shine. Without Piercing, it's very hard to hit 4 cells consecutively. Looks universally useful; only delivers for precision+piercing builds.

**Floor/ceiling**: Beginners never maintain a 4-hit streak. Experts thread the bolt through cell rows for devastating double passes. Piercing stacking makes this protocol scale non-linearly.

**Unlock tier**: Late. Requires understanding of bolt angles, piercing interactions, and wall-avoidance strategy.

### New Protocol E: Burnout

**One-line**: Breaker has a heat gauge that fills while moving (4s to full) and drains while stationary (2s to empty). Full heat: next bump deals 4x damage and fires a shockwave. Standing still for 1.5s: instant full drain + 2s breaker speed boost.

**Strategy shift**: You WANT to alternate between frantic movement and deliberate stillness. Normally you're always moving. Burnout makes standing still a strategic choice -- you stop to cool down, get a speed boost, then resume moving to build heat for the next mega-bump. The rhythm becomes: move-move-move-STOP-speed boost-move-move-MEGA BUMP-stop-speed boost... This is a fundamentally different movement pattern.

**Why it's a rule change**: You WANT to stand still. In this game, standing still is normally death (bolt is moving, timer is ticking, you need to be under the bolt). Burnout makes stationary play a deliberate strategy. The tension: standing still to cool down while the bolt is screaming around the field. Do you stay put for the speed boost, or do you move to catch the bolt and build heat? Every moment is a choice between heat-building and cooling.

**Synergies**:
- **Breaker Speed chips**: Faster movement fills heat faster. But the speed boost from cooling stacks with breaker speed, making the post-cool movement phase explosive.
- **Overclock chip** (bolt speed on perfect bump): If you mega-bump (full heat, 4x damage) with a perfect bump timing, you get the 4x damage + Overclock speed boost + shockwave simultaneously. The skill ceiling for timing this is enormous.
- **Erosion hazard**: Breaker shrinks while you're standing still (not bumping). The stationary cooling phase becomes dangerous because you're losing breaker width and can't restore it (bumps restore width, but you're standing still).
- **Haste hazard**: Faster bolt means less time between bounces. Standing still for 1.5s while a fast bolt bounces around is terrifying -- you might need to catch it before the cooldown completes.
- **Decay hazard**: Timer ticks faster. Every 1.5s standing still costs proportionally more. The cooldown window becomes a race against the timer.
- **Aegis breaker**: Lives-based. Can afford to stand still and risk a bolt-loss for the mega-bump + speed boost cycle. Lives are the safety net that lets you commit to the Burnout rhythm.
- **Chrono breaker**: Time penalty on bolt-lost during the standing-still window is devastating. But the 4x mega-bump helps clear nodes faster, offsetting the time risk.

**Trap potential**: Standing still is terrifying. The bolt doesn't wait for you. Players who stand still at the wrong time lose bolts. The 1.5s stationary requirement for cooling is an eternity in a game where the bolt can cross the field in under a second. Also bad with Haste -- faster bolt gives you less time to stand still safely. Looks powerful (4x damage bumps!) but demands rhythm mastery and bolt-tracking while stationary.

**Floor/ceiling**: Beginners either stand still too long (lose bolts) or never stand still (never cool down, never get mega-bumps). Experts weave standing-still windows between bolt passes, timing cooldowns for when the bolt is safely bouncing in the cell field, then mega-bumping on the return. The movement pattern looks like a martial art.

**Unlock tier**: Late. The "stand still in an action game" concept requires overcoming every instinct the game trains into you.

## New Legendary Chip Interactions

The 5 demoted protocols need synergy and hazard mappings as legendary chips:

### Overburn (Legendary Chip)
- Synergizes with: Bolt Speed (longer trail), Bolt Size (wider aura), Singularity (tiny fast needle = precision trail)
- Anti-synergy with: Momentum hazard (non-lethal ticks grow cells -- DEVASTATING trap)
- Build identity: passive damage aura, rewards speed builds

### Fission (Legendary Chip)
- Synergizes with: Chain Reaction (recursive destruction feeds counter), Cascade (AoE kills count), Entropy Engine (more destruction volume)
- Anti-synergy with: Overcharge hazard (8 bolts + per-kill speed = untraceable bolts)
- Build identity: bolt army, destruction volume

### Iron Curtain (Legendary Chip)
- Synergizes with: Aegis breaker (life-loss triggers), Desperation (speed boost on loss), Last Stand (speed stacking)
- Anti-synergy with: Cascade hazard (pulse feeds heals)
- Build identity: defensive, bolt-loss mitigation

### Kickstart (Legendary Chip)
- Synergizes with: Piercing Shot (devastating opening salvo), Feedback Loop (opening chain), Singularity (nuclear alpha strike)
- Anti-synergy with: Drift hazard (opening angle disrupted)
- Build identity: explosive opener, node-start optimization

### Gravity Lens (Legendary Chip)
- Synergizes with: Bolt Speed/Surge/Overclock (speed = stronger pull), Haste hazard (hazard feeds the chip)
- Anti-synergy with: Drift hazard (competing forces), Echo Cells hazard (ghosts distort center-of-mass)
- Build identity: auto-aim for speed builds

## Why:** These 5 replacements fill gaps the demotions created while strictly passing the "play differently" test. The chip pool gains 5 interesting legendaries. The protocol pool gains 5 genuine rule-changers. Both systems are stronger.
